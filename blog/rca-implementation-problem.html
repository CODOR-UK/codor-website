You don’t have an RCA problem. You have an implementation problem

30 Apr 2026 · Reliability

We’re Good at RCA And Still Failing

I have seen organisations spend weeks if not months of employee time on diligently investigating failures, and yet the failures persist.

The problem is often not a lack of effort, competency or even accuracy of the investigation. Rather it is,

  • Insufficient time, budget and labour to implement the approved recommendations,
  • Sub-standard implementation which does not meet the intent of the recommendation,
  • Little or no verification that the implemented solution actually prevents or mitigates recurrence.

When completing the investigation is treated as success, rather than solving the problem, then the organisation doesn’t realise the value of the effort spent on the investigation. Ten implemented and verified solutions beat one hundred completed investigations every day of the week. If a piece of equipment fails and you can read the investigation from the last time this happened, then the system of investigation is not working.

RCA Doesn’t Create Value. Implementation Does

Investigations are a value creating process only when they focus on implementation and verification, without implementation they only absorb resources.

Recommendations are valueless until they have been tested through application and they are verified as effective. Investigations are theoretical until solutions are implemented and the problem is resolved.

I have conducted investigations before where I was convinced I had the smoking gun, only to find out that my implemented solutions didn’t change the performance in any measurable way. I solved a 10 year compressor problem by being wrong safely and quickly a few times, then correct, implemented and verified once.

The iteration of technically valid solutions yields faster and more effective results than continuous analysis and long implementation timescales. Don’t demand more investigations, demand improved performance and less failures.

The Problem Isn’t the Investigation, It’s What Happens Next

Investigation quality matters, without it the chance of good recommendations is low. The quality unlocks value, it doesn’t directly deliver it.

Ultimately implementation success and problem resolution is the most definitive measure of investigation quality. The goal is high quality investigations, with credible and effective solutions that reduce or remove the risk, which have been implemented.

Unfortunately, the process normally fails at that key transition point between the investigators and the Operations and Maintenance departments. Not out of malice or neglect, but out of real world priorities and limited budgets.

Investigation value dies when,

  • Generic recommendations are left open to interpretation
  • Unrealistic recommendations cannot be executed
  • Recommendations with disparate value wither in a backlog
  • Verification is an afterthought

Too Much RCA. Not Enough Implementation

A focus on investigation closure, rather than problem resolution, creates a culture of excessive recommendation backlogs. This causes long delays between the investigation and implementation, creating time for memories and intent to fade.

If solutions cannot be implemented then why not focus on a few high value investigations at a time, push hard to implement and measure success. Then only allow further investigations once capacity opens up. Focus only on the most impactful failures, use your resource in the field implementing solutions, not in an office conducting excessive investigations.

When investigations clearly drive value and performance recovery, then leaders naturally increase the flow of resource and budget to their execution.

When investigations don’t drive change or improvement, then business leaders become resistant to the resource commitment and the process loses credibility.

Investigations are successful when their benefit doesn’t need to be justified with a presentation.

When Is It Actually Done?

When is an investigation complete?

  • When the investigation is completed by the team?
  • When it is approved?
  • When the actions are closed?
  • 18 months later when the equipment doesn’t fail again?

The purpose of the investigation is to solve a problem, it can’t be complete until that is proven. In most cases verification is the monitoring of a negative i.e. did it not fail when it was expected to.

This contrasts with typical performance metrics which can be easily measured like open investigations or open actions. These are practical metrics for performance management, but alone they do not drive the correct incentives.

Verification repeatedly checks performance against expectations over the long term. I used routine (quarterly) reviews of equipment with implemented solutions that were expected to fail within the next period, then checking whether they have. This is how we proved reliability improvement in a fleet of equipment, implementing improvements and verifying success over time. Then celebrating success once the performance has materially improved.

The investigation team must consider what a successful investigation looks like, especially when writing recommendations. So this will inform how verification can be undertaken later, and provides the means for leaders to hold the implementers to account. If you can’t measure how a recommendation is deemed successful, then you probably should not submit the recommendation.

RCA Is an Experiment, Not a Report

To work in this way requires a clarity of purpose: “we are here to fix the problem, we might not get it on the first try and that’s ok”.

Learning from failure is the direct application of the scientific method. An investigation is the hypothesis, the implementation is the controlled trial, only the measurement of success allows the completion or evolution of the experiment.

In this way the investigation should not be considered sacred, instead it,

  • challenges assumptions
  • focuses on observable or measurable facts
  • adapts to new evidence
  • develops executable solutions
  • evolves when previous solutions didn’t work

Learning from failure is both the learning of what happened, and the learning of how to solve it. When an organisation correctly solves a problem it can re-use that solution, creating exponential value from the initial investment. When they learn what doesn’t work, the value is almost as great.

Not every solution will be perfect, but it will be technically feasible and safe. Expecting this allows the organisation to continuously improve its performance.

Focussing on improving the chosen few will, over time, yield more value than a broad approach of trying to fix everything. It is psychologically difficult to allow some equipment to perform poorly so you can focus on solving another piece of equipment, but if you don’t then neither will ever get solved.

The investigation is the start of the work, not the end. The end is when a solution has delivered value, and is now being used to solve problems on other equipment.

Value comes from performance improvement.

If you are not implementing and verifying solutions, you are not doing RCA. You are documenting failure.

Even when this is executed well, you can extract more value…
