Fixing the first failure is valuable. Preventing the next one is priceless.

4 Jun 2026 · Reliability

The Same Failure, Again

How many times have I seen the same failure mode spring up across different areas of the plant, with a known fix already available? The answer is simple: too many. The comments of “I’ve seen this before” and “how did you fix this over there”, show systemic failure to apply hard won solutions across a fleet. We fought that battle, fixed the equipment, and forgot there was a war to be won.

The investigation was good, the solution worked, but they did not ask “can we use this anywhere else?”. The worst part is the next solve only requires implementation, because the investigation cost was paid on the last failure. Catch the next one before it bites. As an old colleague of mine used to say “never waste a good catastrophe”.

We should be generally intolerant of repetitive events, if we fixed it once then I don’t want to see it again in a slightly different guise somewhere else.

The Value We Fail To Capture

Organisations cannot afford to apply learnings narrowly. By failing to act systematically they commit to multiple failures, multiple investigations and potentially novel solutions each time. This means more cost, lower availability and no scaling of solutions.

Instead, by acting systematically, the company can solve chronic problems with minimal engineering and only the implementation cost. Every application lowers the cost of implementation and increases the likelihood of success, making the solution business as usual.

It is clear why in the real world this doesn’t occur. Performance management is driven by the need to close specific actions, nobody wants the “never ending action” to implement a solution across 30 instances. Engineers are incentivised to “get actions closed”, not “fix as much as you can”, this is compounded by large investigation backlogs.

In practice, repeat events are common and rarely identified as true repeats. The value remains on the table, and worse it’s not even obvious that it is there.

Success here looks like an investigation team identifying that these problems and solutions could apply to a fleet of equipment, recognising that this instance might be just an indication of a larger problem. Therefore, recommendations should be designed to apply widely, they are merely proven on the first instance. Fleet reliability improvement is always the target, fixing only one piece of equipment is a position you settle for.

Why the System Produces Local Solutions

Most investigation processes are inherently focussed on acute problems, not sharing learnings. Companies do not incentivise the transition of solutions from one asset to another.

Investigations are a response to a singular failure, with a known impact, with scrutiny from those directly impacted by the loss. People are hardwired to address experienced pain rather than preventing future pain. So once the immediate problem has a solution, we switch focus to the next immediate problem, even if its impact is less than the chronic impact of the previous failure mechanism.

Recommendations need to be funded and executed by discrete resources, which forces them to be assigned to singular assets. Leaders in those areas only want to see their areas fixed, as that is what they are accountable for. Leaders in other areas of the business will generally see external “suggestions” or “recommendations” as additional work they don’t have the resource or budget for, and as such a nuisance.

This all drives a culture of focussing only on your own area, even if your experience is valuable to other areas. This is not malicious, it is not incompetence, it is the natural result of the incentives which are built into a siloed system.

I have seen this in practice during a meeting of an asset team. The asset team were discussing a particularly impactful valve failure, which had indicated a wider chronic issue with this design. The Ops manager asked the obvious question: “how do we stop this happening again?”. The response was essentially that we couldn’t, which was unpalatable at best, we just have to wait for the next failure to take us offline.

The solution was to identify which design elements drove the root cause, then which other equipment was at risk. The solution applied in the first instance could then be applied directly or adapted to the other equipment in a pro-active manner, preventing further downtime and availability impacts.

Thinking Beyond the First Failure

When equipment fails we witness one of the many failures which can occur on that equipment class, under those operating conditions. Each piece of equipment will have a dominant failure mode which obscures the other ways it could fail, which when solved will expose the next one.

Multiple components experience degradation simultaneously for different reasons of design and operation, and the quickest degradation rate becomes the dominant failure mode, triggering the wider repair and reset of all failure modes (the equipment overhaul). This is the basis of Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA). In this context an investigation is an opportunity to deep dive a particular failure mode and really understand how it works in practice, and what caused it to be dominant.

So rather than asking “does this apply more widely”, you could ask “what justifies this being unique”. If there are comparable duties and equipment elsewhere we should assume shared failure modes, and shared solutions.

We think of equipment based on its function within the wider process, and how its failure impacts the plant, which makes it feel very specific. In investigations we also review physical, human and systemic causes, which creates a high degree of specificity. This encourages acute thinking, which locks in narrow solutions.

This is why RCA targets systemic causes, because those causes by their nature create solutions which apply most widely. Companies focus on “reaching systemic” but that link to the “why” (because it generates system wide recommendations) is often missed.

Solutions can be expanded across two categories:

  1. The equipment class (i.e. pump, turbine, compressor)
  2. The operational duty (i.e. crude oil, water, natural gas)

The widest solutions may cover a whole category, the most narrow will apply at the intersection between the two.

So while narrow causes are ideal in investigations, wide solutions are optimal in execution.

The Obvious Objections

All business activity is in competition for resource, so sharing learnings has to be justified. Here are some examples of typical trade-offs:

  • Not every failure is replicable or has wider transferability, some failures are genuinely unique. The point is to reach this conclusion through analysing the possibility of shared learnings, not as a base assumption.
  • We don’t have the capacity to review which other equipment might be affected, we can only just manage the investigations. What matters is whether more value is gained from extracting the shared learnings from a single investigation or conducting multiple investigations. There are cases where either can be true, the main thing is to consciously ask this question and identify when the shared learnings hold greater value.
  • Managing shared learnings is hard, we are a big organisation and we struggle with sharing information. Frankly it is more typical that organisations only struggle to share specific types of information, critical safety alerts are mostly shared quickly, accurately and with verification of receipt. The better question is whether the value of sharing the information is well communicated, particularly with business leaders. When sharing learnings is incentivised or expected from the top, the business will find a way.

Changing the Expectations

Shared learnings need to be driven in two places simultaneously, the first is within the investigation team as a core part of the investigation deliverables, the second is the leaders who expect the question of broader applicability to be explicitly answered.

Once the investigation team has a thorough understanding of the root causes and potential solutions they should understand the key variables which allow the failure to occur. This also tells them where else this issue could occur.

In the drafting of recommendations they should consider whether the recommendation can actually prevent every instance of this failure occurring. This is not always possible, sometimes each solution needs slight tweaking and engineering specifics, but a 75% overlap is better than nothing and still valuable to other assets and/or sites.

Recommendations should in this way be split between specific local recommendations and broader learnings to be shared. These may be direct recommendations of what to change, or guidance on where to start looking and what to look for.

Once learnings begin to accumulate, the investigation team might start by first asking whether something like this has occurred somewhere else before, what happened and whether any of the recommendations or learnings can be applied. Some validation might be needed to understand if there are any differences, particularly where previous learnings were already implemented.

Leaders need to understand the value that can be derived from sharing learnings, which is already visible in areas like safety. They will then have clear expectations that the applicability of shared learnings are reviewed and explained within any investigation report.

They should then champion these learnings amongst their peers across the site or portfolio, this also means promoting and actioning the learnings received from their peers.

The incentivisation and recognition of a strong knowledge sharing culture is core to facilitating this activity. This should be a fundamental benchmark of investigation effectiveness within the organisation.

Learning from Failures You Never Had

The ultimate value of shared learnings is being able to prevent failures without ever having to experience them directly. The majority of the cost of any solution is the operational downtime and failure you had to experience to discover it. Therefore, every time you can apply a learning from someone else you can eliminate the majority of the cost while realising most, if not all, of the benefit.

This is how the high maturity sites in a portfolio can be leveraged to support the lower maturity sites. Those lower maturity sites are the most in need of learnings and solutions, but also have the least capacity to generate them. When higher maturity sites can share their learnings, other sites can use this as a means to leap forward without having to spend years learning lessons the organisation has already paid for.

Shared learnings as a source of value is already well understood conceptually, and already rigorously applied in different disciplines and sectors. A great example is the sharing of high consequence failures in industry, particularly those that had major safety or environmental impacts. Most people have experience of this, and it is not contentious in any sense. In fact we expect it as part of having a mature safety culture.

Amongst vendors who provide reliability improvement contracts, this is also a big source of commercial advantage. By pooling failure data, investigations and solutions from across industries and companies the vendor is able to leverage previous learnings to improve reliability much quicker. A single site instead has to experience every specific failure directly first.

The vendor's site representative can leverage the vendor's total experience across every site in its portfolio to solve the problems on the single site. Some learnings are directly transferrable, others provide a foundation from which to build accelerated improvements for a fraction of the true cost.

In this sense we can see that taking the same expectations we have in the safety discipline and applying it to reliability investigations we might begin to leverage portfolio learnings in a way that mirrors those companies that specialise in selling reliability improvement as a service.

What Good Looks Like

So what does all this mean?

Solving investigations accurately and implementing solutions is valuable, applying those learnings across your fleet to prevent future failures is priceless.

If we look at a typical manufacturing process, the cost of a day's downtime might be multiples of the cost of repairing the failure. The value is in preventing the downtime occurring, and every time the same learning can be used to prevent a further failure there is exceptional payback value.

The first implementation and validation proves that the solution works and addresses the root causes as identified. This demonstrates value to other stakeholders in the business and helps convince them to invest their own energy in applying that learning, they can see the direct benefit to themselves. This is the foundation of effective knowledge sharing.

Knowledge sharing based only on un-validated investigation reports, where value has not been demonstrated, is unlikely to succeed. When the improvement is proven and material, people want to know how you did it. Validated investigations can explain exactly how they provided value, this is the means by which that value might be replicated. The value of a learning is determined by how many failures it prevents, not how accurately it explains the first one.

One challenge still remains. Once these learnings have been identified and validated, where should they live? For many organisations, investigations and FMEAs exist as separate activities. However, if both are trying to understand and manage the same failure modes, perhaps they are not as separate as we think. I'll explore that idea in the next article.
